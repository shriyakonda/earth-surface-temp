Ground Truth

How hot is the ground beneath our feet? While air temperatures dominate the news, 21 years of NASA satellite data reveal the unshielded thermodynamic realities of a warming planet.

DSC 106 Final Project · Spring 2026

What you're looking at

You're looking at readings from Terra, a NASA satellite that has been measuring the temperature of the ground itself. Not the air, but the sand, rock, forest canopy, and asphalt every month for 21 years. Land surface temperature is what the planet is actually radiating, and it catches things that weather stations miss: the heat of remote deserts, scorching parking lots, and forests that have no one keeping track. And for two decades, this data has been quietly accumulating in NASA archives, mostly unread by anyone outside a research lab.

A single month is just a snapshot. But air temperature records are collected at weather stations, which are unevenly distributed, often located near cities, and sheltered from direct sun. The ground is not sheltered. To see whether anything has actually changed, you need to look at the moments when the heat stopped being a weather event and started becoming a body count. Air temperature lagged. Ground temperature did not.

The comparison tells you what changed. But color scales make it easy to miss a degree here, two degrees there. Those small numbers are not small. The anomaly map strips away the color baseline entirely and shows only the shift itself: where the planet used to recover and no longer does, where the climate buffer has collapsed, and where the ground is simply hotter now than it was twenty years ago.

The warming is not uniform. The Sahara, the Middle East, South Asia: places where people work outside, where air conditioning is not an option, where a two-degree shift in ground temperature is the difference between a hard summer and a deadly one. From space, 21 years compress into a single view. The pattern is not a line on a chart. It is geography.
