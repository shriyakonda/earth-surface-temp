What you're looking at

You're looking at readings from Terra, a NASA satellite that has been measuring the temperature of the Earth every month. Terra specifically measures the temperature of the ground itself — sand, rock, forest, canopy, ice — which is different from air temperature. This distinction matters because land surface temperature is what the planet is actually radiating. We can use it to predict weather, storms, and evaporation, and see things that ground stations miss entirely: the temperatures of remote stretches of desert, or scorching parking lots where no one is keeping track.

Project Writeup

What we've done so far

We used NASA's MODIS MOD11C3 dataset on monthly land surface temperature with 5km resolution and converted raw values to Celsius and masked invalid pixels such as zeros, oceans, and sensor errors as NaN. We then reduced the size of the dataset from 26 million cells to 400,000 cells, and converted NaN values to JSON. We then pulled 4 time periods: July 2020, January 2020, July 2003, July 2024, and built an HTML5 canvas renderer with a blue to red color scale and added a temperature legend. Finally, we added two working interactions: the button to switch between different time periods, and a hover tooltip with temperature and coordinates.

What will be the most challenging part to design

We anticipate the most challenging part of this project will be turning a map with buttons into a real explorable explanation with a narrative driving the visualizations, instead of the visualizations driving the narrative. We also anticipate that writing substantial explanatory text to properly explain the changes and capture the reader's attention will be something that is also difficult. Additionally, we need to decide if we want to add more years, anomaly maps, and be careful of our framing while doing it. As tough as the data is here, we also need to make sure our design matches the narrative we are trying to display.
