Ground Truth

21 years of watching Earth's surface temperature from space.

DSC 106 Final Project · Spring 2026

What you're looking at

You're looking at readings from Terra, a NASA satellite that has been measuring the temperature of the ground itself — not the air, but the sand, rock, forest canopy, and asphalt every month for 21 years. Land surface temperature is what the planet is actually radiating, and it catches things that weather stations miss: the heat of remote deserts, scorching parking lots, and forests that have no one keeping track.

About this project

Ground Truth uses NASA's MODIS MOD11C3 dataset — monthly land surface temperature composites at 5km resolution, spanning 2003 to 2024. Raw HDF files were downloaded via NASA's earthaccess library, converted from Kelvin to Celsius, and downsampled from 26 million to 400,000 cells for browser performance. The visualization is built entirely in vanilla JavaScript using HTML5 Canvas for the maps and Three.js for the 3D globe, with no mapping library dependencies.

What this means

The warming isn't uniform. The Sahara, the Middle East, South Asia — places where people work outside, where air conditioning isn't an option, where a two-degree shift in ground temperature is the difference between a hard summer and a deadly one — are warming faster than anywhere else.

Ground Truth puts 21 years of that data in your hands. Not as an average. Not as a projection. As the actual heat of the actual ground, in the months when it mattered.

What you can do

  • Stay informed. NASA Climate tracks these changes in real time.
  • Support heat relief. The Red Cross runs cooling centers and emergency aid during heat events.
  • Advocate locally. Urban heat hits low-income neighborhoods hardest — cooling access and green infrastructure are policy issues, not just environmental ones.
